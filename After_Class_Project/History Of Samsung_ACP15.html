SAMSUNG

Samsung Group is the largest chaebol (family-run business conglomerate) in South Korea. Its historical evolution spans from a local grocery trader to a global tech powerhouse. Below is the exhaustive, deep-dive history of Samsung structured by distinct eras, corporate strategies, and technological shifts.

1. The Foundation & Trading Era (1938-1968)

  • The Dry-Goods Start (1938): Founder Lee Byung-chul launched Samsung Sanghoe in Taegu (now Daegu), Korea, with a starting capital of 30,000 won (roughly $25 USD). The company operated a small grocery store with 40 employees, primarily exporting dried fish, local vegetables, and noodles to Manchuria and Beijing. The name "Samsung" translates to "Three Stars," chosen by the founder to symbolize something big, numerous, and everlasting.
  • Post-War Industrialization (1950s): Following the devastation of the Korean War, Lee Byung-chul moved headquarters to Seoul and actively sought to rebuild the domestic economy. He opened a sugar refinery (Cheil Jedang) in 1953 and the nation's largest woolen textile mill (Cheil Mojik) in 1954.
  • Financial and Retail Consolidation (1960s): Leveraging protectionist government policies designed to foster local industry, Samsung aggressively diversified. It acquired three of Korea's largest commercial banks, a department store, insurance firms, a fertilizer plant, and an oil refinery

2. The Electronic Pivot & Domestic Dominance (1969-1987)

  • Entering Tech (1969): Samsung Electronics Co. Ltd. was formed. The move was initially unpopular with domestic competitors who feared market cannibalization. The newly formed division partnered with Japan's Sanyo to manufacture low-cost consumer electronics.
  • The Appliance Wave (1970-1979):

    • ~ 1970: Manufactured its first 12-inch black-and-white vacuum tube television.

    ~ 1971: Achieved its first major international export, shipping 500 television sets to Panama.

    ~ 1974: Developed South Korea's first frost-free refrigerator (SR-180) and acquired a 50% stake in Korea Semiconductor, cementing its long-term bet on silicon chips.

    ~ 1979: Mass-produced its first VHS-compatible VCRs.

  • Heavy Industry Shift (1970s): In response to national economic blueprints, Samsung established Samsung Heavy Industries and Samsung Shipbuilding, which went on to build mega-tankers and industrial transport units.
  • Early Compute and Telecom (1980–1983): In 1980, Samsung acquired Hanguk Jongja Tongsin, pivoting into telecommunications switchboards and telephone systems. In 1982, it manufactured its first personal computer, the SPC-1000.

3. Semiconductor Breakthroughs and "New Management" (1988–1999)

  • The Leadership Transition (1987): Founder Lee Byung-chul passed away. His son, Lee Kun-hee, assumed the chairmanship. He restructured the conglomerate, spinning off several major non-core family branches (including CJ Group and Shinsegae).
  • The Memory Chip Supremacy:

    • ~ 1983–1985: Samsung built its first semiconductor fabrication lines, defying global skepticism by rapidly scaling up production from 64K DRAM to 256K DRAM.

    ~ 1992: Samsung shocked international markets by developing the world's first 64-Megabit DRAM chip, overtaking Japanese and American firms to become the permanent global leader in memory chip production.

  • The 1993 "New Management" Declaration: Shocked by poor build-quality and internal inefficiencies during an international retail tour, Lee Kun-hee gathered top executives in Frankfurt, Germany. He famously ordered: "Change everything except your wife and children."

    • ~ To demonstrate this shift, he ordered the physical bonfire destruction of $50 million worth of defective mobile phones and fax machines in front of 2,000 factory employees, resetting Samsung's corporate philosophy to focus on absolute product quality rather than quantity.

  • Mass Infrastructure Projects: Throughout the 1990s, Samsung's engineering and construction branch (Samsung C&T) built massive global skyscrapers, including the Petronas Towers in Malaysia (1998) and later the Burj Khalifa in Dubai.

4. The Digital Revolution & Mobile Hegemony (2000-2019)

  • LCD Panel Supremacy (2000–2005): Samsung invested heavily in Liquid Crystal Displays (LCD), becoming the largest flat-panel TV manufacturer globally by 2005, displacing Sony.
  • The Galaxy & Smartphone Era (2010): While Samsung made early mobile devices in the late 1980s (like the SH-100), the turning point arrived with the deployment of Google's Android operating system.

    • ~ In 2010, Samsung launched the Samsung Galaxy S, featuring a high-performance Super AMOLED screen and its proprietary Hummingbird processor.

    ~ By 2012, Samsung officially surpassed Nokia to become the world’s largest mobile phone manufacturer by sales volume, a crown it fought to hold with Apple for over a decade.

  • Aggressive Acquisitions: To scale its hardware capabilities, Samsung spent billions buying up foreign software and hardware ecosystems, notably acquiring SmartThings (IoT platform) in 2014 and Harman International Industries (automotive and audio technologies) for $8 billion in 2016.

5. Modern Era, Folding Tech, and Leadership Challenges (2020–Present)

  • The Foldable Revolution (2019–Present): Facing market saturation, Samsung focused heavily on display innovation, establishing the modern foldable smartphone industry with the launch of the Galaxy Fold.
  • The Semiconductor Foundry Wars: To diversify away from volatile memory chip pricing, Samsung expanded its Foundry Business. It invests hundreds of billions to compete directly with Taiwan's TSMC in manufacturing next-generation sub-3nm system microchips for external tech clients.
  • Leadership Transition (2022): Following years of legal battles, pardons, and temporary leadership vacuums after the death of Lee Kun-hee in 2020, his son Lee Jae-yong (Jay Y. Lee) was officially appointed as the Executive Chairman of Samsung Electronics in late 2022, marking the third generation of family leadership.

6. Structural Breakdown of the Present Empire

Today, Samsung operates as a loose network of completely separate corporate entities tied together by shared history and family cross-shareholdings:

Major Subsidiary Primary Industrial Focus
Samsung Electronics Smartphones, DRAM/NAND Memory, Foundry, Displays, Home Appliances.
Samsung C&T Corporation Global Construction, Engineering, Fashion, & Energy Traning
Samsung Heavy Industries Mega-scale commercial ship building and offshore drilling rigs.
Samsung Life Insurance South Korea's largest domestic life insurance and asset management firm.
Samsung Biologics Massive contract biologic medicine development and manufacturing (CDMO).
