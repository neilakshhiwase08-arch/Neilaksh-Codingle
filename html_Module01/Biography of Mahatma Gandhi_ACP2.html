Information

Mohandas Karamchand "Mahatma" Gandhi (1869–1948) was an Indian lawyer, anti-colonial nationalist, and political ethicist. He led India to independence from British rule using nonviolent resistance. Revered as the "Father of the Nation", his philosophy of Satyagraha inspired global civil rights movements.

Quick Facts

Born: October 2, 1869

Porbandar, Gujarat, India

Died: January 30, 1948 | New Delhi, India (Assassinated)

Spouse: Kasturba Gandhi

Core Philosophy: Satyagraha (Truth-force/nonviolent resistance)

Early Life & Education

Born into a devout middle-class family, Gandhi was raised with strong values of vegetarianism, fasting, and religious tolerance. In 1888, he traveled to London to study law. Upon returning to India, he found limited success setting up a law practice in Bombay.

The South Africa Years (1893-1914)

In 1893, Gandhi accepted a one-year legal contract in South Africa. There, he confronted severe racial discrimination and apartheid-like segregation.

He spent 20 years organizing the Indian community to peacefully protest restrictive laws.Evolution: It was during this period that he developed his hallmark philosophy of Satyagraha—a nonviolent approach to achieving social and political change.

The Indian Independence Movement (1915-1948)

Gandhi returned to India in 1915 and quickly emerged as a prominent leader of the Indian National Congress (INC). He traded his European clothes for simple, homespun garments (Khadi) to identify with India's rural poor.

Key milestones of his leadership include:

The Salt March (1930): A monumental 240-mile nonviolent protest where Gandhi and thousands of followers walked to the Arabian Sea to make their own salt, directly defying the oppressive British salt tax.

Boycotts and Self-Reliance: He championed Swadeshi (the use of Indian-made goods) and non-cooperation movements to cripple British economic and political institutions.

Quit India Movement (1942): Initiated the final major civil disobedience campaign demanding an immediate end to British rule.

Assassination & Legacy

Gandhi's vision for an independent India included Hindu-Muslim unity. While India finally gained independence in 1947, the country was simultaneously partitioned, resulting in devastating communal violence. He spent his final months fasting and working for peace between the two religious factions. On January 30, 1948, he was assassinated by a fanatic who opposed his ideals of coexistence.

His philosophies, principles, and methods of nonviolence cemented his legacy as a global icon for peace, heavily influencing subsequent leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela

Personal Philosophy & Experiments

Dietary Trials: He spent decades experimenting with food, strictly eating uncooked fruits, nuts, and seeds at one point.

Celibacy Tests: In his later years, he practiced Brahmacharya (celibacy) and controversially slept naked with young women, including his grandniece, to test his spiritual self-control

The Ashram Lifestyle: He founded community settlements like Tolstoy Farm in South Africa and Sabarmati Ashram in India, where all members performed manual labor and shared communal assets.

Conclusion

Power of Nonviolence: He proved that passive resistance (Satyagraha) could destabilize a global empire without firing a shot.

Flawed Humanity: His life demonstrates that historical icons can hold deeply contradictory personal views while still achieving monumental historical progress.

Enduring Blueprint: His philosophy shifted global protest strategies, proving that moral authority can overpower military and economic might.
